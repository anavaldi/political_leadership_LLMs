# Political Leadership and LLMs: How Personal Traits and Gender Stereotypes are Reproduced in LLMs' Political Representations


As large language models (LLMs) increasingly shape public discourse about politics worldwide, understanding how these systems represent political leadership across diverse cultural contexts becomes critically important. This research examines regional and gender biases in LLMs' representations of political leadership through a masked language modelling paradigm. Using a two by two comparative design, that is male/female and Global North/Global South comparison, the study analyses how four LLMs associate leadership dimensions and personal traits across these demographic categories. Results reveal significant regional variations, with ``performance-oriented`` leadership more strongly associated with Global South leaders and ``dynamic`` traits with Global North leaders across multiple models. Gender analysis demonstrates persistent stereotypical patterns, with ``face-saving`` consistently associated with female leaders and varying gender associations for ``visionary`` leadership depending on model architecture. Intersectional analysis shows that gender stereotypes manifest differently across regional contexts. These findings suggest that LLMs encode and potentially amplify existing biases in political leadership representation, with implications for Western-centricity in AI systems and broader sociopolitical impacts as these technologies increasingly mediate public understanding of global leadership.
